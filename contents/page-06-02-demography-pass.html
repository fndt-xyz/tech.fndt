It's not just about selling laptops; it's about selling the service to prolong the lifespan of IT devices and the expertise to utilize computing power to the fullest.

It is the same: whether a high-end 2nd hand laptop is chosen or a mediocre brand-new one on the same budget, a push to full potential is required. Even if a cheaper, second-hand mediocre device becomes the go-to, exactly how to maximize the hardware despite its limitations is a known parameter.

That is what is sold.

Break the flow and change the habit by switching to open-source operating systems and software. If the old way of working isn't taking the business anywhere, a new way to get things done is needed. 2nd hand hardware can give the business a genuine refresh; by giving a device a second chance, business fatigue caused by bloated, standard systems is cleared out. Shaking up the software—using a complete open-source stack—removes the burden of the old workflow. That is the true meaning of refreshing with old hardware.

If the business is in a transformation phase or training staff for a new chapter, it makes sense to bet on low-risk, 2nd hand hardware paired with open-source systems. This is the smartest way to implement open-source into business processes, from productivity suites to ERPs. Utilizing 2nd hand laptops or mini-PCs allows for the development of self-contained workflows or dedicated machines for specific tasks without the high cost of new equipment. It’s a low-cost entry into a high-performance way of working.

The target audience for 2nd hand hardware consists of:

  • The Power User: One who knows exactly what these machines can do and seeks an open-source environment where hardware is pushed to the absolute limit.
  • The Urgent Need: One in a dire situation who requires functional tech immediately. Help is provided to get the device running without the "brand-new" price tag.
  • The Deal Seeker: One who seeks the best value. This endeavor acts as a conduit to hardware "as-is" for a fair price. No cut is taken and no profit is made from the hardware; only upfront processing costs are charged to ensure quality and delivery.
